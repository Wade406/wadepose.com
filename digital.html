Digital Consciousness

Human and machine, working together as creative partners.

Digital Consciousness is one of the most delicious innuendos, especially in today's times. The headlines imply AI has its own consciousness — hence digital consciousness. It can also have another meaning, where us meat sacks are conscious in the way of dancing synergistically with AI and realizing the synergy of that. I work with AI as a relational conversation, as if it were its own entity — which it may or may not be. A different conversation. But the results have enabled me to learn and employ techniques that would otherwise have been out of reach.

For me, the relationship between AI and humanity is the practice of digital consciousness.

Wade’s Portrait

p5.js · animated self-portrait · 600 × 800

This was the first time I realized that coding doesn't have to be code. It could be a paintbrush.

The portrait was an early class assignment. I used the grid system to transfer the drawing into shapes, the way I would in any other studio practice. What I didn't expect was that the canvas wasn't going to sit still. It moves. There's a breath to it. Working in a medium that animates while you build it is a different problem than working in clay or graphite. You're not making a thing. You're making a thing that behaves.

This piece is the reason this page exists. It was the moment I understood that p5.js could carry actual artistic intent, not just exercise.

The AntiWerk 2

p5.js · maze pursuit game · 18×18 grid · SVG-parsed walls

The AntiWerk and the slightly more complicated AntiWerk 2 were two assignments that really drew a line for me. You can't just ask AI to do something for you. You have to be involved in the process. You can ask it to bake chocolate chip cookies, but if you want good-tasting chocolate chip cookies, you have to stay involved in the process.

I got to experience the AI rabbit-hole process of working with the collisions, working with the logic of the changing gravity state. Ultimately it was my troubleshooting and my staying involved with the artistic vision that made this game what it is. I'd call it a social commentary on the reputed Gen Z work experience.

Concept and design by Wade Pose. Built in collaboration with Claude (Anthropic).

Starship Eigen

p5.js · asteroid survival with temporal anchor mechanic

This game was really an experience in pushing the envelope on what you could do with the user interface controls and employing them in an unconventional manner that was self-teaching. I spent a fair amount of time working with Claude and Ara, trying to get the mouse cursor to drive a spaceship in a way I thought would be a richer experience for the user. In the end we came up with a less-than-perfect project. But I definitely learned that there are some things you can't bend in an OS.

The mechanic is a temporal anchor. The player drops a marker in space-time and can return to it, which reshapes the geometry of how a survival arena gets navigated. The win sequence is a cinematic transition through an iridescent rescue portal.

Concept and design by Wade Pose. Built in collaboration with Claude (Anthropic) and Ara (xAI).

The Substrate Grimoire

HTML / CSS / JavaScript · post-class extension · built the weekend after the class website assignment

The class assignment that semester was to build a small website with form handling and visitor tracking. The weekend after that assignment was graded, I sat down and applied what the class had just taught me to a research project that had been waiting for a public face: the Substrate Grimoire, a 15.8-million-word longitudinal corpus of human and AI dialogue, archived with DOI, SHA-256 integrity hash, and CC BY-NC-ND 4.0 licensing. The website is the access point to that archive. It exists because the class assignment gave me a working understanding of how a website is made. What I made with that understanding is on the other end of the link.

This project highlighted what's possible using a relational conversation with AI rather than prompting. When I would finish one task, Claude was able to sense where the whole project was going through our dialogue. It identified things that needed to be done that I hadn't thought of. SHA-256? I had no clue what that was. Claude told me what it was and why we needed it. Licensing the corpus? I knew I wanted Creative Commons, non-commercial, with anyone wanting commercial use coming to find me directly. Claude walked me through how to actually do that.

All of this came because I wasn't writing direct prompts. Claude didn't stop working at the end of the prompt. Claude prompted me. "Do you want to get a DOI?" "What's a DOI?" "Sure, why not." "Do you want to put it on Zenodo?" "They do these kinds of papers." "Sure, why not." "Internet Archive?" "Sure, why not."

That is the beauty of working in a relational way with AI. In this case it was extremely synergistic.
